Man-in-the-middle attacks: What they are and how to prevent them

An Overview of Man-in-the-middle attacks

What are man-in-the-middle attacks?

A man-in-the-middle attack is carried out by hackers to insert their presence in between the communication of two parties to gain access to all the information sent to and from both the parties. The hacker can stop the users from sending and receiving data, or might even divert and redirect the messages to another user. The main objective of Man-in-the-Middle attack is to eavesdrop the users’ conversation, masking their presence, making it appear so normal as if there is no third person involved in the communication.

How do they work?

Sniffing: Sniffing or Packet Sniffing is a technique used to capture the packets of data flowing in and out of a system/network. Packet Sniffing in networks is equivalent to wiretapping in telephones.

Packet Injection: In this technique, an attacker injects malicious packets of data along with regular data. This way a user doesn’t even detect the malicious files because they come as a part of a legitimate communication stream. These files are a common commodity in man-in-the-middle attacks as well as denial-of-service attacks.

SSL Stripping: SSL Stripping or SSL Downgrade attacks are a rare type when it comes to MiTM attacks, but also the most damaging one. As we all know, SSL/TLS certificates keep our connection safe online via encryption. In SSL Stripping attacks, attacker removes the SSL/TLS connection and the secure HTTPS protocol is changed to insecure HTTP protocol.

How to prevent man-in-the-middle attacks?

These attacks are not easy to prevent as they are highly complicated in nature. You need to have some technical skills and the right tools must be used. But just because a MiTM attack is difficult to defend against, doesn’t mean you shouldn’t actively protect against them.

Examples:
- Make sure that the websites you visit have HTTPS in front of the URL
- DO NOT make a purchase or send sensitive data on a public Wi-Fi network.
- Don’t click on malicious links or emails
- Secure your home/work network

Denial of Service Attack: What it is and how to prevent it

An Overview of DoS attacks

What is a denial of service attack ?

A denial-of-service attack is a security event that occurs when an attacker prevents legitimate users from accessing specific computer systems, devices, services or other IT resources. Denial-of-service (DoS) attacks typically flood servers, systems or networks with superfluous requests in order to oversaturate the target's capacity and make it tough or impossible for legitimate users to access them.

A variant of Dos known as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack occurs in much the same way as DoS attacks except that requests are sent from many hosts instead of just one, illustrated in DDoS attacks often involve many “zombie” machines, which have been previously compromised by the attackers. These “zombie” machines then send massive amounts of requests to a service to disable it.

How much damage can it do to systems and services?

In early 2000, a Canadian high school student Michael Calce shut down Yahoo! with a distributed denial of service (DDoS) attack. Other sites as Amazon, CNN and eBay also have been successfully disrupted by Calce.

Obviously not the first DDoS attack, but that highly public and successful series of attacks transformed denial of service attacks from minor nuisance to powerful business disruptors in the minds of CISOs and CIOs forever.

Since then, DDoS attacks are commonly used to take revenge, conduct extortion, as a means of online activism, and even to start cyberwar. They have also been growing over the years. In the mid-1990s an attack may only consisted of 150 requests per second. Nowadays, they can exceed 1,000 Gbps. This has largely been powered by the sheer size of modern botnets.

How to prevent it?

A DDoS attack is practically impossible to prevent due to being on the receiving end. However, the chances of getting these attacks can be reduced through some key information security practices, including performing ongoing security assessments to search for and patch Denial of Service related vulnerabilities and using network security controls.

In addition, an organization's contribution to DDoS attacks across the internet can be minimized with email phishing testing, solid patch management practices and regular network monitoring and alerting.
